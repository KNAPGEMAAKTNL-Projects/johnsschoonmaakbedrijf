### 1. Energy Level — Calm Authority vs. Bold Confidence

> I found that **[Vebego](https://www.vebego.nl/cleaningservices)** uses a restrained, neutral palette with quiet confidence — no bold taglines, no flashy elements, just professional imagery and people-focused messaging that says *"we're so established, we don't need to shout."* Meanwhile, **[CSU](https://www.csu.nl/)** leads with a bold, punchy tagline — *"Scherper. Slimmer. Schoner."* — backed by animated counters, an innovation narrative, and green/lime accents that radiate energy and forward-thinking.
>
> Both are top-rated: [Vebego](https://www.vebego.nl/cleaningservices) is the #2 largest company in the Netherlands; [CSU](https://www.csu.nl/) is #1 in the MT500 ranking. But they attract different clients. **Vebego's calm authority** appeals to institutional decision-makers (hospitals, airports) who value stability. **CSU's bold energy** appeals to companies that want a partner who innovates and challenges the status quo.
>
> **For John's Schoonmaakbedrijf — which energy matches your ideal client?** Are your best customers the ones who value *steady, dependable, no-nonsense service*, or do they want to feel they're working with someone *sharp, modern, and ahead of the curve*?
I would say steady generally.

### 2. Imagery Approach — Your Team in Action vs. The Clean Result

> **[TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)** features real action photography throughout — their actual cleaners at McDonald's, their team in branded uniforms, a group photo, a "day in the life" video. You see the *people doing the work*. In contrast, **[Housekeep](https://www.housekeep.com/)** shows the *outcome* — clean rooms, fresh kitchens, sunlit living spaces. You rarely see a cleaner; you see the result of their work.
>
> [TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)' approach builds trust through *transparency* ("look, these are real people, and here they are at recognizable clients"). [Housekeep](https://www.housekeep.com/)'s approach sells a *feeling* ("imagine your space looking like this"). Both achieve high engagement — [TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/) scores 9/10 in design with strong local loyalty; [Housekeep](https://www.housekeep.com/) has 52,000 monthly visitors and 300,000+ reviews.
>
> **For John's — should the photography center on John, Egbert, and Benny doing the work** (builds the personal, local, "you know who's coming" trust), **or should it focus on the sparkling end result** (sells the outcome and lets the quality speak)? Or a mix — and if so, what's the ratio?
Trust for sure. We will get new pictures here as well.

### 3. Trust Signaling — Earned Subtlety vs. Proud Display

> **[EW Facility Services](https://www.ew.nl/)** takes a subtle approach to trust: client logos (Holland Casino, Concertgebouw) are placed mid-page without fanfare, no star ratings anywhere, no "best of" badges. The design itself *is* the trust signal — it looks so polished that credibility is implied. This works for EW because their clients are enterprise-level decision-makers who find loud self-promotion off-putting.
>
> **[TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)** does the opposite: their 9.6/10 rating appears in the hero, mid-page, AND near the footer. "328,562 ramen gewassen" is displayed as an animated counter. 24+ client logos scroll past. 5 named testimonials each have their own section. It's proud, loud, and data-heavy — and it works because their clients (local businesses) need *proof before they pick up the phone*.
>
> **John's has VCA certification since 2007 and 17 years of client relationships — powerful assets.** Should these be presented with quiet confidence (a small badge, a few select logos, "let our work speak"), or should John's go bold with it ("Al 17 jaar vertrouwd door X bedrijven in de Betuwe" with counters, badges, and prominent ratings)?
I would say since this website will be mainly focussed on companies (some smaller than the other but okay), the preference is in clean confidence. Talking about experience doesn't have to scream, which is something ew does well. They have text about their 30 years of experience.

### 4. Information Density — Breathe & Discover vs. Everything Upfront

> **[EW Facility Services](https://www.ew.nl/)** keeps its homepage clean: a video hero, a value proposition, a few service links, client logos, and a CTA. It's spacious, elegant, and trusts visitors to click deeper. You feel guided, not overwhelmed. They scored 2,300 monthly visitors with a design rating of 8/10.
>
> **[TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)** packs 15+ sections onto one page: hero, rating badge, team photo, 12-service carousel, brand story video, client logos, "why us" pillars, guarantee messaging, 5 testimonials, animated stats, team section, charity partnerships, blog previews, FAQ accordion, AND a contact form. It's comprehensive — everything a prospect needs to decide is on one page, no clicking required. 2,900 monthly visitors, 9/10 design.
>
> **For a Betuwe client visiting John's site — would they prefer a calm, guided experience** where they discover information across a few clean pages, **or should everything they need to know be on the homepage** because they're likely comparing 2-3 local options and won't click deeper?
More towards topcleaners

### 5. Interaction Style — Solid & Still vs. Alive & Responsive

> **[Vebego](https://www.vebego.nl/cleaningservices)** is essentially static — no animations, no counters, no scroll effects. It uses card-based modular design and strong photography, and the stillness feels intentional: *stable, reliable, institutional*. For a company managing 8,000 employees across cleanrooms and airports, motion would feel frivolous.
>
> **[CSU](https://www.csu.nl/)** brings the page to life: stat counters animate on scroll ("X+ klantlocaties"), cards have hover effects, there's a CO₂ barometer tool, navigation responds with smooth megamenus, and video plays in modal overlays. The animation serves the "innovation" brand — it feels *modern and forward-moving* without being distracting.
>
> **For John's website — should it feel solid and grounded** (clean photos, clear text, no moving parts — reflecting dependable, traditional service), **or should it have subtle moments of life** (a counter ticking up years of experience, smooth hover effects on service cards, maybe a short video autoplay in the hero)? What impression do you want visitors to walk away with in the first 3 seconds?
More towards vebego.

### 6. Contact Approach — One Clear Path vs. "However You Prefer"

> **[Schoonmaakwoede](https://www.schoonmaakwoede.nl/)** funnels everyone through one path: "Bereken prijs" (Calculate your price). You enter your details, get an instant price, and book. The phone number and email exist but are secondary. This works because their service (home cleaning) is standardized and bookable — the client knows what they want; they just need a price.
>
> **[Succes Schoonmaak](https://www.successchoonmaak.nl/)** offers multiple paths: "Offerte aanvragen" button in the header, a phone number (prominently displayed), email, a careers section, and a video that builds relationship before asking for action. This works for B2B commercial cleaning because every client has a different need — some want a quick call, some want a formal quote, some want to evaluate the company culture first.
>
> **John's serves B2B commercial clients.** A facility manager at a school in Culemborg and a factory owner in Tiel likely have very different decision-making styles. **Should John's website offer one clear funnel** ("Offerte aanvragen" everywhere — keep it simple), **or multiple entry points** (phone to John directly, WhatsApp, email, quote form, maybe even a "plan een kennismaking" option)?
Yeah no for johns there is no standard process yet for this, so not standardized and bookable.

### 7. Hero Strategy — Statement vs. Proof vs. Benefit

> Three top-performing approaches I found:
>
> **Statement-led ([CSU](https://www.csu.nl/)):** *"Scherper. Slimmer. Schoner."* — A bold brand tagline as the first thing you see. No explanation needed; the confidence itself is the message. Works when the brand is already known.
>
> **Social-proof-led ([TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)):** The hero immediately shows their **9.6/10 rating badge**, a team photo, and the CTA. Before reading a single word of copy, you see the number. The proof IS the pitch. Works when you're competing against alternatives and need to win on trust instantly.
>
> **Benefit-led ([Succes Schoonmaak](https://www.successchoonmaak.nl/)):** *"Voor een schonere wereld"* — Leads with the result and the mission, followed by their three pillars (people, wow moments, partnership). Works when you want to communicate values and build an emotional connection.
>
> **John's has been in business since 2007, is VCA-certified, and serves the Betuwe.** When a potential client lands on the homepage, **what should hit them first?** A confident statement (*"De schoonmaakpartner van de Betuwe"*), a trust signal (*a 9+/10 rating or "17 jaar ervaring" counter*), or a benefit (*"Zodat u zich kunt richten op uw bedrijf"*)?
More towards the confident statement.

### 8. Service Presentation — Browse at a Glance vs. Deep Expertise Pages

> **[Schoonmaakwoede](https://www.schoonmaakwoede.nl/)** presents services as simple, bookable packages on the homepage — 3 main options, clear pricing, select and go. There are no deep-dive service pages. This works because home cleaning is a simple, understood product.
>
> **[CSU](https://www.csu.nl/)** takes the opposite approach: 10+ industry-specific branch pages (healthcare, education, offices, industry, logistics...) each with dedicated content, case studies, and tailored messaging. A hospital procurement manager and an office facility manager see completely different pages. This positions [CSU](https://www.csu.nl/) as *specialist* rather than *generalist* — and it drives massive SEO value.
>
> **For John's — the current site lists 45+ location pages but no service-specific pages.** You mentioned services likely include bedrijfsschoonmaak, glas- en gevelreiniging, onderwijsschoonmaak, industriële schoonmaak, kantoorschoonmaak, and dagschoonmaak. **Should these be presented as a quick visual overview on the homepage** (6 cards with icons, 2-sentence descriptions), **or should each service have its own dedicated page** with specific photos, process description, and a tailored CTA? The dedicated pages approach is more work but would dramatically improve SEO for searches like "kantoorschoonmaak Tiel" or "glasbewassing Culemborg."
Yeah its own dedicated pages. It is for companies and they need to know how we work.

### 9. Personality — The Brand vs. The Founder vs. The Team

> **[CSU](https://www.csu.nl/)** is a brand: no individual faces in the leadership, no founder story, no "meet John." The identity is the company, its innovations, its awards. This works at €602M in revenue — the brand transcends any single person.
>
> **[Succes Schoonmaak](https://www.successchoonmaak.nl/)** is a family-led brand: they feature a 3-part leadership video, emphasize the "Volendammer mentality," and tell their origin story from 1963. The family IS the brand — but at 500+ employees, it's blended with corporate professionalism. You trust the family values AND the institutional scale.
>
> **[Vebego](https://www.vebego.nl/cleaningservices)** takes a people approach: individual employee spotlights (Jörgen the Schiphol window washer, Desiree in train cleaning). It's not about the founder or the brand — it's about the actual workers you'll interact with.
>
> **John's is small (4 full-time, 1 part-time) with named team members (John Hak, Egbert van Zijl, Benny Mulder).** This is a genuine advantage over faceless competitors. **How visible should John be?** Should the site be a personal brand (*"John's Schoonmaakbedrijf — persoonlijk, vakkundig, betrokken"*), a team story (*"Maak kennis met ons team"*), or a professional brand that happens to be small (*"Uw schoonmaakpartner in de Betuwe"* — no specific faces, just capability)?
More a team story.

### 10. Aspiration Level — Best in the Betuwe vs. Playing National

> **The data tells a clear story.** The best local competitor website in the Betuwe (Clean Up Tiel) scores a 6.5/10 with 6 monthly visitors. The national leaders like TopCleaners score 9/10 with 2,900 visitors. Housekeep operates at 9.5/10 with 52,000 visitors but is a tech platform — a different league entirely.
>
> There are two strategic choices:
>
> **Option A — Best in the Betuwe.** Build a site that reaches [TopCleaners](https://topcleaners.nl/)-level quality (real photography, prominent rating, "Offerte aanvragen" 4x, client logos, VCA badge, team video). This would make John's the undisputed #1 cleaning website in the entire region — immediately outclassing every local competitor. It's achievable, realistic, and would drive meaningful business results.
>
> **Option B — Signal national quality, deliver local.** Go beyond what any regional competitor attempts — custom visual identity, story-driven homepage, lifestyle photography, animated trust counters, online quote tool, blog content strategy, service-specific SEO landing pages. This positions John's not just as "the best local option" but as a company that looks like it *belongs* alongside [CSU](https://www.csu.nl/) and [Succes Schoonmaak](https://www.successchoonmaak.nl/) — while delivering the personal, direct service that only a small local business can.
>
> **Option B costs more and takes longer, but it creates a moat:** once built, no local competitor could match it without similar investment. **Which ambition level matches your goals for John's Schoonmaakbedrijf?**
Option A is good.
